Why Raccoons Are a Problem in New River

Understanding raccoon problems New River homeowners face helps recognize early warning signs and appreciate the value of professional wildlife management. New River's location at the desert-mountain interface creates exceptional habitat diversity supporting robust raccoon populations. These adaptable mammals thrive wherever water, food, and shelter coincide‚Äîconditions New River provides abundantly through residential development, agricultural operations, and proximity to Agua Fria River and Cave Creek corridors.

Entry points raccoons exploit in New River homes include roof intersections where different planes meet, soffit areas along roof edges, chimney openings without proper caps, attic vents with damaged screens, and foundation gaps near utility penetrations. The area's older homes often feature wood shake roofs, decorative architectural elements, and generous eave overhangs that raccoons readily manipulate. Once raccoons establish entry points, they enlarge openings through persistent tearing and gnawing, creating damage that worsens rapidly and compromises structural integrity.

Raccoon health risks demand serious attention from New River homeowners, as these animals carry numerous diseases transmissible to humans and pets. Rabies remains the most concerning threat in Arizona, where raccoons represent a primary rabies vector species alongside bats and skunks. Additional health hazards include raccoon roundworm (Baylisascaris procyonis), a dangerous parasite found in raccoon feces causing severe neurological damage if accidentally ingested. Leptospirosis, giardia, and various bacterial infections also spread through contact with raccoon urine, feces, or contaminated surfaces.

  • Nocturnal sounds - Heavy scratching, thumping, chattering, or crying noises in attics or walls after dusk
